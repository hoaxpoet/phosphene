Cream & charcoal

Four plates exploring the territory opened by Cathedral Lights. Two with cream/white as a primary structural colour (15—16) and two with black/charcoal as a primary structural colour (17—18).

Cathedral Lights established cream as structural highlight in a deep-jewel-dominant field. Cycladic and Ming Porcelain invert that — cream as ground, saturated colour as accent. Tenebrism and Obsidian push the dark-dominance direction symmetrically.

Cycladic

Santorini, Mykonos, Naxos — Greek island whitewash. Limewash, Crete sand, and Aegean foam do the cream-primary structural work; Aegean cobalt and Greek flag blue ground the deep end; bougainvillea, hibiscus, and lemon punctuate. The library’s first true cream-as-ground palette — the inverse of Cathedral Lights, where saturation was ground and cream was highlight.

Ming Porcelain

Jingdezhen kilns, 14th–18th century. The famous cobalt-and-white underglaze tradition with later famille rose and famille verte overglaze additions. Jingdezhen porcelain and pale celadon as ground; underglaze cobalt and sang-de-boeuf carry the deep saturation; imperial yellow, famille rose, peach bloom animate the mid-register. Quieter than Cycladic — Eastern, disciplined, ceramic. Brings sang-de-boeuf, a copper-glaze oxblood unlike any red elsewhere in the library.

Tenebrism

Caravaggio, late 1590s onward. The Italian Baroque discovery that black ground plus dramatic warm light equals unprecedented emotional weight. Caravaggio black and charcoal void hold the panel; vermilion robe, lapis drapery, wine madder, and brass armour carry the saturated mid-register; candle flame gold and tallow cream are the highlights where light falls on skin and wax. The library’s first explicitly figurative-painterly palette — every cell has a referent in oil on canvas.

Obsidian

Volcanic geology — Iceland’s Reykjanes, Mexico’s Popocatépetl, Hawaii’s Kīlauea. Four dark anchors hold the panel (obsidian glass, basalt, ash, glacial obsidian); magma orange, sulfur yellow, cinder red, and geothermal blue are the eruption colours. The library’s most aggressively dark-dominant palette and the only geologically-derived one. The hot-and-cold contrast (magma against glacial obsidian) does the work Tenebrism does with warm-on-warm — a different emotional register at the same dark saturation.
