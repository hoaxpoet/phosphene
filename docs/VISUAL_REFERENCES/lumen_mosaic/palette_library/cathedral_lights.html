Cathedral Lights

Chartres, Sainte-Chapelle, Saint-Denis. Twelfth-century stained glass at noon, light coming through the south transept. The deep cobalt-purple known as bleu de Chartres, Sainte-Chapelle’s ruby, Saint-Denis emerald. And — for the first time in the library — the warm cream and ivory and beeswax tones that read as light passing through pale or aged clear glass, the structural highlights that make medieval windows readable as windows.

Project rule change · anti-cream rescinded · 2026-05-17 The "no muted palettes" invariant (CLAUDE.md Visual Quality Floor; LM.9 no-cream gate; D-LM-d5 vividness floor) was a defence against the LM.2 / LM.4.5.x failure mode where pale tones dominated the panel and the result read as washed cream haze. This plate uses pale tones as structural highlight against deep jewel tones — the compositional inverse of LM.2’s `mix(cream, hue, sat)` formula. Cream cells exist; they cannot exceed ~25 % of the panel without losing the cathedral register. The new discipline is compositional, not categorical. Cathedral Lights

Seven deep saturated jewel tones do the heavy work — Chartres blue, Sainte-Chapelle ruby, Saint-Denis emerald, manganese violet, pot-metal amber, iron-oxide rose, copper green. Three pale lights — cream, beeswax honey, pearl ivory — render as light through aged clear glass rather than as washed surface. Sky pane gives one cool pale accent. Lead line anchors the deep end and reads as the lead came between panels.
