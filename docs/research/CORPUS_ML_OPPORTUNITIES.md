# The 400GB Corpus: ML Opportunities for Phosphene

**Date:** 2026-07-07 · **Status:** Research analysis (not an increment; untracked — commit is Matt's call) · **Survey completed 2026-07-07** (second session; volume readable at `/Volumes/Extreme SSD`)
**Input:** Matt's personal music archive (~400GB MP3/M4A/FLAC, `Letter/Artist/[Year] - Album` layout). Measured: **27,639 real tracks** (20,375 MP3 / 4,502 M4A / 2,762 FLAC), ~1,085 artists, ~2,104 albums, est. **~1,940 hours**. Full survey results in the Appendix.

## 1. The core asymmetry: scale without labels

Every shipped Phosphene model was trained on a corpus dramatically smaller than this archive. Beat This! trained on 4,556 annotated tracks across 17 datasets; the MoodClassifier's DEAM source is 1,802 45-second excerpts; Open-Unmix's MUSDB18 is 150 songs; the Harmonix Set (structure ground truth) is ~912 tracks. At 27,639 measured tracks, Matt's archive is six to a hundred times larger than any of these — but it carries no beat annotations, no stem ground truth, no valence/arousal ratings, no section boundaries.

That asymmetry structures everything below. The corpus's value is **not** as a drop-in supervised training set. Its value comes in four forms: (a) **full-length audio** where production is structurally capped at 30-second previews and 10-second stem windows; (b) **in-domain distribution** — this is literally the music Phosphene's user listens to, so calibration and domain adaptation on it target the deployment distribution exactly; (c) **pseudo-labelable raw material** — modern teacher models can annotate it automatically at usable quality; and (d) **a real-audio regression bank** — Failed Approach #27 already establishes that synthetic audio cannot substitute for real-music pipeline noise, and 400GB is the definitive supply of real music.

One constraint frames all of it: **"Learning stays local"** (Development Constraints) governs runtime telemetry, not development. Training or fine-tuning offline on Matt's own hardware, on Matt's own files, then shipping frozen weights through the existing MPSGraph weight-extraction path (the same path that produced the Beat This! and Open-Unmix tensors) is fully consistent with D-009 — which D-170's reversal explicitly clarified means "no CoreML," not "no ML."

## 2. Full-track analysis on local files — the highest-leverage unlock

The LF path (D-129) decodes the same PCM bytes it plays, but the analyzers silently truncate: StemSeparator sees ~10 s, Beat This! sees ~30 s (`tMax = 1500` frames). D-129 is explicit that the LF.2 win was structural, "not full-track analysis." The corpus makes full-track analysis worth building, because it supplies thousands of tracks that would immediately benefit and a test bed to validate on.

Chunked Beat This! over the whole file — already named in D-170's SECDET.5 notes as "the heavier alternative if a tempo-varying track ever needs it" — turns the cached BeatGrid from a 30-second-plus-extrapolation object into an exact whole-track grid. Two consequences follow. First, **the cold-start phase problem disappears on the LF path**: the Cold-Start Phase Contract retired automated live phase derivation but explicitly sanctioned "full-track local-file analysis" as one of the fundamentally different premises. With a whole-track grid computed from the same bytes being played, beat phase is *known*, not estimated, from frame 1 — no `LiveBeatDriftTracker` convergence window, no wrong-phase accents. This is the sanctioned premise, and the corpus is the environment it pays off in. Second, whole-track grids are the substrate section detection needs (D-170's live test 1 failed on exactly this — the 30 s grid cap).

Since this touches the cold-start contract, the premise change should be surfaced to Matt before scoping — this document is that surfacing.

Cost note: chunked inference at ~30 s per window means a 4-minute track needs ~8 windows; at warm-predict speeds this is seconds per track, amortized in the persistent SHA256-keyed cache (LF.3), so it's a one-time cost per file.

## 3. A supervised section / key-moment model — D-170's named successor

D-170's reversal is precise about the path not taken: unsupervised DSP peaked at F@3 ≈ 0.58 offline and 0.29–0.41 live; "feels aligned" needs ~0.70+, "which requires a supervised model... ported to MPSGraph (a Beat-This!-scale effort)." The reversal also names the structural blocker: streaming exposes only 30 s previews, so section detection could only ever serve local files. The corpus changes the economics on both halves.

On the model: the strongest published candidate is **All-In-One** (Kim & Nam, WASPAA 2023) — joint beat, downbeat, section-boundary, and functional-label (intro/verse/chorus/bridge/solo/outro) prediction on demixed audio, state-of-the-art on Harmonix Set with a modest parameter count. Its shape is strikingly compatible with Phosphene: it consumes source-separated spectrograms (Phosphene already runs stem separation), and it is a transformer of roughly Beat This! scale, portable through the same manual-MPSGraph route (RMSNorm/SDPA/RoPE workarounds already exist in `BeatThisModel`). Functional labels are exactly the "key moments" ask — and the survey sharpens this: the corpus is rock/alternative/indie-dominant with electronic at only ~5%, so "key moments" here means **choruses, builds, and dynamic-contrast entrances, not EDM drops** — the functional-label vocabulary (chorus/bridge/solo) is precisely the right one for this library. Meanwhile Harmonix Set's pop/rock skew is a reasonable domain match for pretraining. And `sectionSuitability` (25% of `DefaultPresetScorer`'s weight) plus `DefaultTransitionPolicy`'s structural-boundary trigger are already wired to consume boundaries with confidence values.

The corpus's roles here, in order of value: **domain adaptation and evaluation** (run the pretrained model across a few thousand corpus tracks; have Matt spot-check boundary quality on tracks he knows intimately — his ear on his own music is a higher-quality eval than F@3 on SALAMI-IA's live recordings); **semi-supervised fine-tuning** (pseudo-label the corpus with the full model, keep high-confidence tracks, fine-tune the compact port on them — standard noisy-student practice); and **a hand-annotation seed** (annotating even 50–100 personally-known tracks with section boundaries is a weekend of work and produces an in-domain golden eval set that no public dataset provides).

On the structural blocker: see §6 — the corpus itself partially dissolves the "streaming-only-30s" objection.

## 4. Beat This!: evaluation and calibration first, fine-tuning second

Fine-tuning Beat This! requires beat annotations, which the corpus lacks and which are expensive to create by hand. The honest ordering:

**Calibration is immediate and cheap.** `assessBeatIrregularity` (D-154) was calibrated on **38 cached tracks**, and the 10% threshold "sits in a thin observed gap (9.2 vs 11.3) — tune as data accumulates." Running the existing full-mix-vs-drums dual-grid analysis over a few thousand corpus tracks turns that thin gap into a real distribution: where the octave-folded disagreement histogram actually has mass, whether 10% is a natural valley, and how bar-confidence covaries with it. Same story for the 3-way BPM disagreement logging (`SessionPreparer+WiringLogs`) — at corpus scale it becomes a systematic error census by genre rather than anecdotes. This requires zero new ML — just a batch harness driving the existing pipeline.

**Evaluation via ensemble agreement.** Pseudo-ground-truth from agreement between independent trackers (Phosphene's Beat This! port, the reference PyTorch checkpoint, madmom's DBN tracker) identifies the disagreement tail — the corpus tracks where the port diverges from the reference reveal porting bugs; tracks where all trackers disagree with each other are the genuinely hard cases worth listening to. This also directly serves D-154's stated open problem: **swing feel is invisible to the current gate** ("So What's estimators agree perfectly... yet a metronome pulse isn't that song's pulse"). The survey confirms a real jazz shelf (~3% ≈ 800 tracks) plus a classical wing including complete Beethoven piano-sonata cycles — rubato and swing material the current gate has never been calibrated on. That is the development set for a swing/microtiming signal — e.g., inter-onset-interval ratio distributions against the grid — that no 38-track cache could support.

**Fine-tuning, if ever, comes last.** The CPJKU training code and annotation formats are public. If ensemble evaluation reveals a genre cluster where the port systematically fails (and Matt cares about that cluster), pseudo-label + fine-tune + re-extract weights is mechanical. But run the census first; the model may not need it.

## 5. The MoodClassifier is the weakest shipped model and the easiest win

A 3,346-parameter MLP trained on DEAM, consuming 10 hand-picked scalars, with a **known cross-path defect**: the 44.1 kHz (LF) vs 48 kHz (tap) sample-rate delta shifts spectralCentroid ~22% and propagates to valence +34% / arousal −38% — absolute mood is currently not comparable across paths. Mood is 30% of the preset scorer's weight, the largest single factor, driven by the weakest model. Three tiers of corpus-powered response, cheapest first:

**Tier 1 — calibration.** Decode a corpus sample at both rates, run both feature paths, fit the per-feature correction (likely close to a centroid rescale). Kills the cross-path skew with no retraining. The survey confirms the corpus is the right instrument: 86% of files are 44.1 kHz natively (the LF rate), with a 12% 48 kHz minority and a small hi-res tail — resample pairs are trivially manufactured from any of it. Also: z-score scaler parameters (`mood_scaler.json`) came from DEAM's distribution; re-estimating feature means/stds on the corpus recentres the classifier's operating point onto the music it actually sees — which the survey says is 90s–2000s rock/alternative/indie, about as far from DEAM's excerpt pool as a Western catalog gets.

**Tier 2 — re-train the same architecture on better labels.** DEAM is small, Eurovision-era-pop-skewed, and rated by crowdworkers. A teacher-student pass — run a modern music-emotion or embedding model (e.g. a MERT- or CLAP-class model) over the corpus, distill its valence/arousal estimates into the same 10-feature MLP — keeps the shipping architecture byte-compatible (same Accelerate path, same weight arrays, existing golden-fixture test regenerated) while training on 20,000+ in-domain tracks instead of 1,802 excerpts.

**Tier 3 — a richer track-level profile.** The corpus enables computing *per-track mood trajectories* offline (mood over the full track, not the preview average), which is exactly what "matching presets to key moments" needs — the arousal ramp into a chorus is a planning signal `TrackProfile` cannot currently represent. This extends the cache schema, not the runtime classifier.

## 6. The corpus as a streaming-session sidecar — creative but real

D-170's reason #1 for removal was that streaming sessions have no full track to analyze. But a large fraction of what Matt streams plausibly *exists in this archive*. A metadata matcher (artist + title + duration tolerance, with acoustic fingerprint confirmation if wanted) that resolves a streaming `TrackIdentity` to a local file would let the session preparer run **full-track analysis for streaming playback** whenever the track is in the library: whole-track BeatGrid, section boundaries, mood trajectory — all cached under the same identity and installed at track start exactly like today's preview-derived data. The streaming path's 30-second ceiling stops being universal; it becomes the fallback for tracks Matt doesn't own. This single idea retroactively strengthens the case for §2 and §3, because their outputs stop being LF-only. It is also pure local computation — no cloud, no telemetry, fully inside the privacy constraints.

## 7. Stem separation: teacher-student distillation

Open-Unmix HQ is a 2019-era architecture; the quality frontier (HTDemucs and successors) is substantially better but too heavy for a 5-second real-time cycle. The corpus can't provide stem ground truth, but it enables the standard workaround: run a heavyweight teacher over corpus tracks offline, treat its outputs as pseudo-stems, and train/fine-tune a shipping-size student on in-domain material. Two applications, one cheap and one expensive. Cheap: **pre-analyzed stems for owned tracks** — via the §6 sidecar, cache teacher-quality stem *features* (not waveforms) per track offline; the live separator still runs, but `stemEnergyBalance` and stem-affinity scoring (25% of scorer weight) get teacher-grade inputs. Expensive: distill a better student to replace Open-Unmix under the same MPSGraph window contract — real work, only worth it if stem-routing quality is a felt limitation in M7 reviews.

## 8. A golden corpus for regression and preset development

Independent of any model change, the archive should yield a curated **regression corpus**: a few hundred tracks stratified by genre/tempo/era/dynamics (plus known pathological cases — rubato, swing, 7/4, beatless ambient), with the full analysis pipeline's outputs snapshotted. Every future DSP or model change diffs against it. FA #27 requires real audio for diagnostics; D-154's calibration table was built on 38 tracks because that's what the cache held — this makes the real-audio bank a first-class fixture. It also serves preset development directly: the preset session checklist's production-pipeline testing obligations get a standing, diverse, *legally owned and locally present* audio library to pull from, and `RENDER_VISUAL=1` contact sheets can be generated against consistent tracks across sessions.

The same batch harness enables a **preview-representativeness study**: for owned tracks, compare the 30-second preview window's TrackProfile against the full track's. That quantifies how much the orchestrator's plan quality is bounded by preview bias (does the preview systematically miss the chorus? overstate arousal for tracks with loud intros?) — currently an unmeasured article of faith in the session-preparation design.

## 8.5 External metadata enrichment — fingerprint first, then join the frozen databases

The corpus can also be enriched from outside, and the timing quirks of the external-database landscape (verified 2026-07-07) happen to favor exactly this archive.

**Step 1 is identity, not features: Chromaprint → AcoustID → MusicBrainz IDs.** Run `fpcalc` over all 27,639 files and resolve each to a MusicBrainz recording ID. This is the join key for every database below, it disambiguates versions acoustically (the exact requirement §9.5(c) raised — a remix stops being confusable with the album cut), and it is the same fingerprint infrastructure the §6 streaming-sidecar matcher needs. One pipeline, three consumers. `beets` automates the whole layer off the shelf. Privacy note: AcoustID lookup sends fingerprints (not audio) to a public service — a one-time dev-side batch, but if even that is unwanted, the fully-local alternative is matching tags against the MusicBrainz dump directly.

**Step 2: join the AcousticBrainz frozen dump.** AcousticBrainz stopped collecting in 2022, but the complete dataset — ~7.5M recordings, MBID-keyed, freely downloadable — carries BPM, BPM histograms, key/scale, danceability, onset rate, loudness, dynamic complexity, and high-level mood vectors. The freeze is normally the fatal caveat; here it barely bites: this archive is a 90s–2000s collection ripped years ago, so its contents predate the freeze and coverage should be strong. (The same freeze means TC 29-era discoveries get nothing — enrichment serves the archive, not new music.)

**Step 3: genre and era from the living databases.** MusicBrainz, Discogs, and Last.fm APIs remain alive; their canonical genres/styles/tags fix the survey's known mess — 13% missing genre and free-text noise ("Simply Genius," "Mr. Hyde Records") — turning the §8 golden-corpus stratification and the §4 per-genre error census into queries instead of hand-curation. Last.fm's crowdsourced mood tags additionally supply weak labels at scale.

**What enrichment is for — and what it is not for.** Phosphene should not *adopt* external BPM: the shipped Beat This! port is a stronger estimator than the Essentia-derived values these databases hold (Essentia is literally what Spotify's now-dead audio-features endpoint derived from; Spotify deprecated `audio_features`/`audio_analysis` in November 2024 with no official replacement, which forecloses the one API that would have been tempting). The external values' worth is that they are *independent*: (a) a free extra voter in the §4 ensemble-agreement census — octave-fold and flag tracks where Phosphene disagrees with both the reference checkpoint and the database consensus; (b) auxiliary training targets for the §5 Tier 2 mood retrain (AcousticBrainz mood vectors + Last.fm tags alongside teacher-model distillation, three weak sources beating one); (c) **key/mode ground truth at corpus scale for the existing estimator** — `ChromaExtractor` already runs Krumhansl-Schmuckler key estimation, and production code comments flatly call it unreliable (`VisualizerEngine+Audio.swift`: "key estimation is unreliable"); database key values across thousands of matched tracks turn that adjective into a measured accuracy figure and a per-genre failure map, the precondition for ever letting key drive palette selection; (d) clean strata everywhere. Treat every external value as evidence, never ground truth.

## 9. What the corpus cannot do, and one caution

It cannot supply human beat/section/emotion annotations (only seed them), stem ground truth (only pseudo-stems), or **preset-preference labels** — "which preset fits this track" is a taste judgment only Matt's usage can generate. The path there runs through fixing BUG-015 (the live-adaptation wiring that is implemented but never invoked) and logging dwell/skip/nudge signals locally; the corpus then supplies the *feature side* of a personalization dataset whose *label side* accumulates from sessions. That is a later chapter, and it is the one place where "learning stays local" bites at runtime — any preference learning must remain on-device, which it naturally can (the scorer is a 4-term linear blend; even its weights could be tuned locally).

The caution: training models on commercially released music — even owned copies — and shipping the resulting weights in an MIT-licensed public repo is a legally gray area distinct from personal-use analysis. Corpus-derived *calibration constants and evaluation results* are unambiguous; corpus-*trained weights* in the public repo deserve a deliberate decision. (Distilling from teachers that were themselves trained on licensed data, like the public Beat This! checkpoint, is the industry norm and lower-risk.)

## 9.5 The Tunes Club catalog — a human sequencing signal after all

Matt supplied the [Tunes Club Catalog](https://docs.google.com/spreadsheets/d/1RWeeO-L9yBNW0NTATsdL_jUaTvvL354dnLVuDoF_tK8/edit?usp=sharing) (2026-07-07): track listings for a 15-year series of collaborative listening sessions. The exported tab covers **TC 11 (2011) through TC 29 (2026)** — 19 instances, ~106 sets, ~470 ordered tracks, 11 curators (5 core, 6 occasional). Format: each participant brings a hand-sequenced "set" of 4–8 tracks (~25 min); sets play back-to-back over an evening. (TC 1–10's track listings are lost — Matt confirmed 2026-07-07 — so TC 11–29 is the complete recoverable record.) This partially fills the gap §9 and the survey appendix flagged: the corpus's `.m3u` files are rip artifacts, but *this* is genuine human sequencing data. Five distinct uses, none of which is "training data" — at n≈470 it is evaluation- and prior-scale, and that is enough:

**(a) An arc-prior evaluation set for the Orchestrator.** Each set is a deliberate 25-minute emotional arc designed by a human for a listening room — exactly the object `SessionPlan` tries to construct. Matt's own TC 27 set even carries hand-entered BPMs and traces a near-monotone descent (125 → 105 → 98 → 119 → 96 → 89 → 83); other curators show recognizable shapes (Kevin's sets: long-form house builds; Ben's TC 28: party ramp). The testable question: run the full analysis pipeline over each set's tracks and check whether human orderings produce systematically smoother / more-shaped valence-arousal-tempo trajectories than random permutations of the same tracks. If yes, that validated trajectory shape becomes a plannable prior — the first empirical grounding the emotional-arc concept would have.

**(b) A deployment-distribution census — and a measured limit on the §6 sidecar.** Phosphene's real sessions look like Tunes Club, not like the archive: one evening spans glitchy ambient pop (Apparat's 2026 *A Hum of Maybe*), cinematic electronica (Felsmann + Tiley with Ry X), melodic house/techno with genuine builds and drops (Kevin's sets — so the "chorus, not drop" reframing in §3 needs this caveat: guest-curated party playlists DO bring drop-structured music), power metal, dark Americana, and LSO-orchestrated art pop (ROSALÍA's *LUX*). Measured against the corpus at artist level: **~72% of Matt's own TC picks have the artist in the archive, versus ~24% for the other curators'** (track-level hit rates will run lower). Translation: the §6 sidecar multiplies value on Matt-curated sessions but most guest-picked streaming tracks fall back to the 30-second-preview path — both paths have to stay strong, and the census quantifies the split.

**(c) Version disambiguation is mandatory for the §6 matcher.** The catalog is dense with remixes, radio edits, and live versions ("Trikk Selva Furia Remix," "Zeds Dead Remix," "Misunderstood (Live)"). Artist+title matching would silently attach the album version's full-track analysis to a structurally different recording — worse than no sidecar. Duration tolerance is the first filter; acoustic fingerprint confirmation is the real fix.

**(d) Continuous DJ mixes are a session shape Phosphene will meet.** One curator (Greg) routinely contributes a single continuous mix ("The Wheel," "Modern Anachronisms," "A Very Good Year"...). That is a 30–60 minute audio stream with no track boundaries: per-track BeatGrid installs, track-change callbacks, and preview-based profiles all assume a boundary structure these sessions don't have. Worth a line in the UX/session model as a known-unhandled input class before it shows up live.

**(e) Micro-ground-truth and taste-cluster seeds.** Matt's hand-entered BPM column (~10 tracks, TC 26–27) is a tiny but personally-verified beat-tracking eval seed. And the catalog's per-curator structure is a labeled taste-cluster dataset in miniature — eleven internally-coherent but mutually-distant taste profiles (including one curator whose own taste swings dubstep → power metal → Americana → EDM-pop across the years). Any future preset-preference personalization (§9) should expect *within-session* taste multimodality, not a single user vector.

## 10. Priority recommendation

Ordered by leverage per unit effort, in product terms:

1. **Batch analysis harness + calibration census (§4, §8)** — no new ML; a CLI that walks the corpus through the existing pipeline. Unblocks everything else and immediately retunes D-154 thresholds and the mood scaler. This is the natural first increment. The §9.5(a) arc study rides on the same harness for near-zero marginal cost: analyze the ~470 Tunes Club tracks, plot trajectory-by-set-position, compare against shuffled orderings.
   1.5. **Fingerprint + enrichment join (§8.5)** — `beets`/`fpcalc` over the corpus, AcousticBrainz-dump and Discogs/Last.fm joins. Cheap, independent of the harness, and its fingerprint layer is shared infrastructure with the §6 sidecar matcher. Delivers the ensemble BPM voter, key-estimator ground truth, and clean genre strata the census consumes.
2. **Full-track BeatGrid on local files (§2)** — chunked Beat This!; exact beat phase from frame 1 on LF sessions. Requires Matt's sign-off on the cold-start premise change.
3. **Mood Tier 1–2 (§5)** — cross-path calibration, then in-domain re-train of the same tiny MLP.
4. **Library sidecar matching for streaming (§6)** — multiplies the value of 2, 3, and 5 across streaming sessions.
5. **Supervised section model (§3)** — the D-170 successor; Beat-This!-scale porting effort, gated on 1–2 landing first. Highest ceiling for "match presets to key moments."
6. **Stem distillation (§7) and preference learning (§9)** — real projects; take them up only when M7 evidence says stem routing or preset selection is the felt bottleneck.

## Appendix: corpus survey results (2026-07-07)

Measured directly on the mounted volume (`/Volumes/Extreme SSD`). Metadata figures from a 500-track random sample read with mutagen; counts are exact.

**Inventory.** Raw enumeration finds 55,203 audio-extension files, but **exactly half are macOS AppleDouble resource forks** (`._`-prefixed twins) — zero-value files that parse as corrupt audio. Real corpus: **27,639 tracks** — 20,375 MP3 (74%), 4,502 M4A (16%), 2,762 FLAC (10%) — across ~1,085 artists and ~2,104 albums in a `Letter/Artist/[Year] - Album` layout. Mean duration 4.2 min → **~1,940 hours**; 5.2% of tracks exceed 8 minutes (long-form/classical/post-rock material for extrapolation-drift testing).

**Quality.** Median bitrate 320 kbps (min observed 128); FLAC is mostly 16-bit CD rips with a small 24-bit contingent; EAC `.log`/`.cue` sidecars confirm bit-perfect per-track rips (no single-image FLACs needing splitting). Sample rates: **86% at 44.1 kHz, 12% at 48 kHz, ~1.5% hi-res (88.2/96 kHz)** — the §5 cross-path calibration has native material at both rates.

**Tags.** Artist/album ~99%, year 93%, genre 87% — good enough to stratify the §8 golden corpus without re-tagging. Genre histogram (free-text, needs normalization): rock + alternative + punk + indie variants dominate at roughly half the corpus; electronic ~5%, jazz ~3%, hip-hop/rap ~3%, folk/country ~2%, classical ~1% (but including complete sonata cycles — dense rubato material), plus shoegaze/post-rock/soul/latin tails. Decades: 1990s–2000s core (~60% of dated tracks), meaningful 1960s–70s shelf, thin 1980s.

**Codec-robustness pairs.** **174 albums exist in two or more formats** (e.g. *Pet Sounds*, *Grace*, *Sea Change*, *Since I Left You* in both MP3 and FLAC) — ready-made pairs for testing analyzer invariance to codec/bitrate, as hoped.

**Hygiene for the §10-item-1 harness** (these will silently poison any batch run that doesn't filter them): 27,564 AppleDouble `._` files; 112 FairPlay-protected `.m4p` (undecodable — skip); 22 `.part` incomplete downloads; 12 `.wav`. The ~197 `.m3u` files are per-album rip artifacts, not curated playlists — the corpus itself carries **no human sequencing signal**. (The Tunes Club catalog, §9.5, later filled this gap from outside the corpus.)

**What the survey changes upstream.** The genre profile confirms the §3 reframing (chorus-detection, not drop-detection, is the key-moment problem for this library); the jazz/classical shelves make the §4 swing/irregularity census concretely resourced (~1,000+ tracks); the 44.1/48 split hands §5 Tier 1 its calibration material; and the estimate that mattered most — is this corpus big enough to be worth building infrastructure for — lands at ~28k tracks / ~2k hours: 6× the largest dataset any shipped Phosphene model was trained on, entirely in-domain.
